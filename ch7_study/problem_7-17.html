PROBLEM 7-17

SN1 substitution and E1 elimination frequently compete in the same reaction.

(a) Propose a mechanism and predict the products for the solvolysis of 2-bromo-2,3,3-trimethylbutane in methanol.

(b) Compare the function of the solvent (methanol) in the E1 and SN1 reactions.

Setting Up the Problem

The substrate is a tertiary alkyl bromide. Methanol is a polar, protic, weakly nucleophilic and weakly basic solvent. Tertiary halide + polar protic solvent → SN1 / E1 conditions, with both pathways sharing a common rate-determining step (ionization to form a carbocation).

The neighbouring carbon (C3) is quaternary — it has zero hydrogens of its own. So E1 elimination cannot place the double bond between C2 and C3. The only possible β-hydrogens are on the methyl groups attached to C2, giving exclusively a terminal alkene.

(a) Mechanism & Products

Both SN1 and E1 begin with the same slow ionization step. The two pathways diverge only after the carbocation has formed.

Step 1 — Ionization (rate-determining, common to both paths)

The polar protic solvent stabilizes the developing ions, allowing the C–Br bond to break heterolytically. A tertiary carbocation forms; bromide leaves with the bonding pair.

The carbocation is already tertiary and reasonably stable. A 1,2‑methyl shift from C3 would just move the (+) onto the symmetrically equivalent neighbouring carbon — same connectivity, same stability — so no productive rearrangement. The two pathways now branch from this one cation.

Step 2a (SN1) — Methanol attacks the carbocation

A lone pair on the methanol oxygen forms a bond to C+, giving a protonated ether (an oxonium ion).

Step 3a (SN1) — A second methanol deprotonates the oxonium

Another solvent molecule plucks the proton off the positively charged oxygen, giving the neutral methyl ether and a methyloxonium ion (CH3OH2+).

Step 2b (E1) — Methanol abstracts a β-hydrogen

Methanol can act as a (weak) base instead of a nucleophile. It removes one of the nine equivalent β-hydrogens on the methyls bonded to C+, while the C–H bonding pair forms the new π bond. C3 has no hydrogens of its own, so a C2–C3 double bond is impossible — only a terminal alkene can form.

Predicted products

Because C3 is quaternary, the usual Zaitsev preference for the more-substituted alkene is silent here — the only possible E1 alkene is the terminal one. In a typical SN1/E1 mixture in methanol at room temperature, the SN1 ether is the major product, with the alkene as a minor product. Heating shifts the balance toward elimination.

(b) Role of Methanol in Each Reaction

Methanol plays three distinct roles overall, and which role is on display differs between the two pathways.

RoleSN1E1
Solvent
(both reactions)		 Polar protic. Solvates and stabilizes the developing Br− and the carbocation in the rate-determining ionization step. This is what makes the slow step possible — without a polar protic solvent, the C–Br bond would not ionize. This role is identical in SN1 and E1.
Nucleophile Yes — an oxygen lone pair attacks the planar carbocation, forming the new C–O bond of the methyl ether. No.
Base Yes, but only in the final proton transfer: a second CH3OH plucks H+ off the oxonium to release the neutral ether. Yes — a methanol oxygen lone pair removes a β-proton from the carbocation, generating the C=C bond.
  • In SN1, methanol is mainly the nucleophile (and the solvent).
  • In E1, methanol is mainly the base (and the solvent).
  • In both, methanol is the polar-protic solvent that stabilizes the carbocation and the leaving bromide — this shared role is why a single ionization step feeds both products.
