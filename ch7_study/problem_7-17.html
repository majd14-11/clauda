Problem 7-17 — Solution & Mechanisms

Solvolysis of 2-bromo-2,3,3-trimethylbutane in methanol

Why this substrate? It’s the textbook example of clean SN1 / E1 competition with no rearrangement and no Hofmann/Zaitsev ambiguity.

7-17The problem

SN1 substitution and E1 elimination frequently compete in the same reaction.

(a) Propose a mechanism and predict the products for the solvolysis of 2-bromo-2,3,3-trimethylbutane in methanol.

(b) Compare the function of the solvent (methanol) in the E1 and SN1 reactions.

SetupRecognize the substrate & reaction type

Before drawing arrows, look hard at the substrate. Three features decide everything:

  1. C2 is tertiary (3°) and bears the leaving group (Br). 3° + a polar protic solvent (MeOH) ⇒ ionizes readily ⇒ SN1 / E1 regime (not SN2 / E2).
  2. Methanol is a weak nucleophile and weak base — exactly the condition that lets a carbocation form and then be intercepted. The word solvolysis means the solvent is doing the chemistry.
  3. C3 is quaternary, so it carries NO hydrogens. The β-Hs that an E1 can remove can only come from the two methyl groups attached to C2 (which are equivalent), so there is exactly ONE possible alkene. That removes the usual “Zaitsev vs Hofmann” headache.
Both pathways begin with the same first step — C–Br ionizes to a 3° carbocation. Once that cation exists, methanol can react with it in two different ways: as a nucleophile (→ SN1) or as a base (→ E1). That branching point is the whole problem.

Step 1Ionization (rate-determining, common to both)

The C–Br bond breaks heterolytically. Both electrons stay with bromine — the curved red arrow shows the bond-pair leaving with Br. C2 is left with an empty p orbital and a positive charge.

Red curved arrow = movement of an electron pair Blue + / − = formal charge
  • This step is slow / rate-determining — it requires breaking a bond without any help from the nucleophile.
  • The 3° carbocation that forms is stable enough to live long enough for methanol to find it (hyperconjugation + inductive donation from six surrounding methyls).
  • kinetics: rate = k[substrate] (first order, hence the “1” in SN1 / E1).

Pathway ASN1 — methanol as nucleophile

Step 2a · Methanol uses an oxygen lone pair to attack C⁺

An oxygen lone pair on methanol drops onto the empty p-orbital at C2, forming a new C–O bond. Because oxygen now has three bonds (to its CH₃, to its H, and to C2), it carries a formal +1 charge — this is an oxonium ion.

Step 2b · A second methanol takes the proton off the oxonium

The oxonium is acidic (loss of H⁺ regenerates a neutral, stable ether). A second molecule of methanol grabs that proton — its lone pair attacks the H, the O–H bond electrons collapse back onto oxygen, and the catalyst-like role of methanol is complete.

Notice that methanol appears twice in SN1: first as a nucleophile (forming the C–O bond), then as a base (removing the extra H from the resulting oxonium).
SN1 product  =  (CH3)2C(OCH3)–C(CH3)3  =  2-methoxy-2,3,3-trimethylbutane (a tert-alkyl methyl ether)

Pathway BE1 — methanol as base

From the same carbocation, methanol can instead use its oxygen lone pair to grab a β-hydrogen (a hydrogen on a carbon adjacent to C⁺). The C–H bond electrons then flow into the empty p-orbital on C2, forming the new π bond of the alkene.

Two arrows in one concerted-looking step:

  1. Arrow ①: O lone pair (methanol) → β-H. Methanol is acting as a Brønsted base.
  2. Arrow ②: C–H bond electrons → into the C–C bond, becoming the new C=C π bond.
In the slide deck this looks identical to E2, but it’s genuinely E1: the carbocation already exists from Step 1. The “base step” here is the second, fast step — it doesn’t appear in the rate law.
E1 product  =  CH2=C(CH3)–C(CH3)3  =  2,3,3-trimethyl-1-butene
Because C3 has no hydrogens, β-elimination toward C3 is impossible. The only β-Hs sit on the two methyls bonded to C2, and those methyls are equivalent — so the E1 gives exactly one alkene. No Zaitsev/Hofmann competition here.

ProductsWhat actually comes out of the flask

Solvolysis gives a mixture of both products — the relative amounts depend on temperature (higher T usually favors elimination) and on the exact solvent system. For (a), you should report:

SN1 product

2-methoxy-2,3,3-trimethylbutane
(an unsymmetrical methyl tert-alkyl ether)

E1 product

2,3,3-trimethyl-1-butene
(only possible alkene — no β-H on C3)

AsideWhy we don’t need to worry about rearrangement here

Carbocation rearrangements happen when a 1,2-hydride or 1,2-methyl shift produces a more stable cation. Let's check that for our cation:

  • The starting cation is already 3° at C2: (CH3)2C+–C(CH3)3.
  • A methyl could in principle migrate from C3 to C2. But the new cation at C3 — (CH3)3C–C+(CH3)2 — is also 3° with the same neopentyl neighbor.
  • So the rearrangement is energetically degenerate — there’s no driving force to do it, and even if it does, the cation is chemically the same species.
That symmetry is exactly why Wade picked this substrate for Problem 7-17: it isolates the SN1 vs E1 partitioning without the noise from rearranged products.

Same molecule, same lone pair — different destination. That’s the entire essence of the SN1 / E1 partitioning, and the reason these two reactions must compete whenever a carbocation forms in a protic solvent.

★Exam-ready takeaways

  • Solvolysis of a 3° halide in a polar protic solvent ⇒ assume SN1 + E1 mixture, never SN2 or E2.
  • The rate-determining step is ionization. After that, products are decided by which of methanol’s two roles wins out.
  • Methanol = nucleophile in SN1 (attacks C⁺), and = base in E1 (grabs a β-H).
  • For this specific substrate: the 3° cation can’t rearrange to anything more stable, and only one alkene is geometrically possible (no β-H on C3) — so the answer is two clean products: 2-methoxy-2,3,3-trimethylbutane and 2,3,3-trimethyl-1-butene.
  • If you’re asked “which dominates?”, remember the rule of thumb: more heat → more elimination (E1); cooler conditions favor the substitution.
